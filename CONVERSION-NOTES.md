# Conversion Notes — Why Each Change

Companion to `CHANGES.md`. Each change cross-referenced to the conversion framework it applies, so the next iteration can preserve intent rather than copy verbatim.

---

## Hero — loss-aversion / pain-anchored (Option A)

**Framework:** Halbert / Hopkins direct-response — lead with quantified pain, end with the resolution. Loss aversion outperforms gain framing for cold paid traffic at roughly 2.5× — Kahneman & Tversky's prospect theory; replicated across decades of split tests.

**Why "$9,000 in Software Bills" in the H1:** The single biggest mistake of the prior hero was that the math lived in supporting copy. Putting the number in the headline forces the reader to engage — they will mentally calculate "wait, $250 × 36 = is that right?" and that's the engagement we want. Same principle as Caples' Lipton tea ad: specific number > generic claim.

**Why the gold H2 lands the ownership payoff ("Nothing on Your Balance Sheet"):** Loss-aversion frames need a resolution; if you anchor pain without offering relief, the reader bounces. The H2 gives them the "what's at stake" answer in one breath.

**Why the eyebrow qualifies revenue tier ($100K–$2M):** Self-selecting headline above the hero filters out tire-kickers in the first 200ms — anyone outside the range bounces, anyone inside leans in. Ogilvy's "long-headline-with-specifics" school: the eyebrow is a pre-qualifier.

**Why trust strip below CTAs:** Reduces perceived risk before the click. Stripe / Klarna / Affirm / Afterpay are trust-by-association badges (Cialdini's Social Proof + Authority). Naming the build window (7–14 days) and scarcity (5 founding spots) reinforces real urgency without manipulation.

## Auto-open modal softening

**Framework:** Direct response — never send cold traffic to a high-ticket checkout on first contact. AIDA breaks when you skip Interest and Desire and go straight to Action.

**What changed:** The $3K Stripe link in the modal was attempting a $3K close on a visitor who'd seen the site for 2.6 seconds. Softening to "Book a Discovery Call" + "See the Live Build" matches where the visitor actually is in the funnel (awareness → interest) and preserves the modal's "coming at you" entrance for branding.

---

## Social proof above the fold

**Framework:** Cialdini's Social Proof — buyers want to see evidence that someone like them already trusted this. Putting it 80% of the way down the page meant most visitors never saw it.

**Why GDK works as the proof:** It's a real, clickable, live system. Not a logo wall. Not a stock photo. Visitors can click and verify capability themselves — which is the strongest form of proof. Boorstin: "The most credible evidence is the kind the buyer can independently verify."

---

## Single CTA path

**Framework:** Single primary action per section. Multiple competing CTAs reduce conversion because they introduce decision fatigue. Hick's Law: time-to-decide grows logarithmically with choices.

**Why "Book a 20-Minute Discovery Call" is the universal primary:** It's the lowest-commitment next step that still moves the prospect toward purchase. A discovery call is a soft yes; a Stripe checkout is a hard yes. Cold traffic almost never commits to hard yes. Sales scripting research (Chet Holmes, *Ultimate Sales Machine*) puts the buying-window distribution at 3% ready now / 7% open / 30% not thinking about it / 30% don't think they're interested / 30% know they're not. The 90% who aren't ready to buy now will book a call — but won't checkout.

## Comparison block redesign

**Framework:** Concrete comparison + numerical anchoring (Tversky). When you make the math visible side-by-side, the buyer's mental model snaps from "is this expensive?" to "this is X% cheaper."

**Why the takeaway line below both panels is bold:** The numbers do the work, but the takeaway closes the loop with a memorable summary the buyer can repeat at their dinner table. "27%–54% cheaper over three years. And you own it forever." is the line they'll quote to their spouse / partner / accountant.

**Why "What you own at the end: $0." beats "you own nothing":** Specific numerical zero hits harder than abstract negation. The reader sees `$0` rendered in their head and the prospect-theory loss feels real.

## "What We Engineer" outcome-first rewrites

**Framework:** Outcome-first copy (Eugene Schwartz's breakthrough advertising) — describe what the buyer gets, not what we built. Every card now leads with operator outcome, not engineering process.

| Card | Old framing | New framing | Why |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Custom-Engineered Website | "Branded as you" | "compound search authority month over month" | Compounding return framing; speaks to long-term operators |
| Integrated Payments | "Money hits your account fast" | "No friction at the close" | Sales-language reframe; "the close" is operator dialect |
| Customer Financing | "Customers say yes to bigger jobs" | "Higher average ticket. Lower customer drop-off." | Two specific operator KPIs |
| Job & Invoice Tracking | "Stop hunting for paperwork" | "Your business runs from a single screen" | Aspirational outcome (one screen) > pain avoidance |
| SMS Automation | "Your phone does the work" | "Sub-60-second lead response... Speed-to-lead is the single biggest predictor of closing rate" | Quantified outcome + authority-anchor (well-documented in HBR / InsideSales) |
| You Own It | "Yours forever" | "all on your balance sheet" | Asset language — speaks to operators thinking long-term |

## Process section Day-X timeframes

**Framework:** Specific > vague. "Days 3–10: Design & Build" is concrete and defensible; "Quickly" is hollow. Specificity boosts trust because vague claims are typical of dishonest sellers.

---

## About → Founder note

**Framework:** Founder-led copy in the operator-to-operator voice. Sales psychology: people buy from people, especially in the $3K B2B service space. The founder note demonstrates the *why* behind the brand, which becomes the brand's moat against commodity competitors.

**Why the note is signed "— Carlos":** First-person attribution is non-negotiable here. Without a signature, founder copy reads as marketing copy ghostwritten for the founder. With it, the reader feels addressed by a real person.

**Why the Velonyx shield is the placeholder visual:** Brand-consistent, never broken. When Carlos provides a headshot, swap in. Premium brand voice does not need a stock-headshot placeholder; the shield is the right interim.

---

## FAQ rewrites — the 3 highest-objection answers

**Framework:** FAQs that anticipate the buyer's actual objections, not generic SEO copy. Andy Crestodina's heuristic: the FAQ should pre-empt every "but..." the prospect will think on the call. If you don't address it on the page, they'll bring it on the call, and the call will be longer and less productive.

| Q | Underlying objection | How the new answer resolves it |
|---|---------------------|---------------------------------|
| What if I don't like the design? | "What if I waste $3K and hate the result?" | Capped revision rounds + "Most builds reach final approval within two rounds" — risk reversal with a concrete number |
| Do I own the system after it's built? | "Am I going to be locked in?" | "yes — fully" + "hand it to your kids" — escalating ownership scope to make the answer unmistakable |
| Why Velonyx vs SaaS? | "Why pay $3K when I can pay $250/mo?" | Three-paragraph answer that reframes the question itself: "do you want to rent forever, or own?" |

---

## Final CTA section — "Build Once. Own It Forever."

**Framework:** Final-CTA copy as a recap of the page's central argument. Robert Bly: "The close should be the headline restated for someone who's now informed."

**Why "If you've made it this far, you've already done the math":** Acknowledges the reader has consumed the page. Creates parasocial trust ("we know you're here, we know what you've read"). Direct response: the reader is rewarded for paying attention by being addressed as smart, not pitched at again.

---

## Banned-word sweep

Every removed banned word (juggling, stitching, patched-together) is a generic marketing tic that pattern-matches to lower-quality marketing copy. The premium positioning we want for Velonyx is "this brand sounds like it's been thought through," and that requires zero generic marketing-speak. Plain language, specific verbs, and operator dialect — that's the voice.

---

## What this overhaul does NOT do

- It does not add fake testimonials. Real testimonials get added when Carlos has real attributed quotes.
- It does not promise outcomes we can't deliver. Numbers used ($9,000 stack cost, 7–14 day build, $250–400/mo current spend, $6,600 3-year cost) are all defensible from the public pricing of the comparison vendors + Velonyx's own pricing.
- It does not add scarcity manipulation. The "5 founding spots" is real (manually tracked dot count on the founding counter) and the post-founding price ($5K + $200/mo) is a real future change documented in CLAUDE.md.
- It does not change the pricing model, motto, payment integrations, or architecture. Only the positioning, copy, and conversion surfaces.

## Round 2 — Founder · Payments · Refund Policy · OG Card · JSON-LD (May 19, 2026)

### OG social card
**Why:** A premium offer that lacks an OG card looks unprepared in DM previews and social shares. The Pillow-generated card is a stop-gap — 1200×630, brand-correct (near-black, gold gradient headline, shield + wordmark), legible at thumbnail size. User can drop a Canva-designed JPG at the same path to override without code changes. *Tradeoff acknowledged: a designer-built card will outperform Pillow output for click-through.*

### Affirm removal
**Why:** Active payment methods need to match what's actually wired into Stripe + the customer financing partners. Listing a payment method that isn't supported burns trust the moment a customer hits checkout and it isn't offered. Affirm was carried over from earlier copy; it's not active in the live Stripe payment link or the financing partner roster. Removing it tightens the promise.

### Founder reveal
**Why:** "From the Founder" with a real bio + signature reads as accountable. The prior 4-paragraph version was more rhetorical ("renting is a lie...") — emotionally strong but didn't establish *competence*. The new bio leads with the credential that matters for a custom-engineered platform pitch: "Background combining technical engineering with operational discipline — building production cloud systems, payment integrations, and SMS automation infrastructure." That's the founder version of "we know how to build the thing we're selling you." Paragraph 2 names the pain in operator language. Paragraph 3 places the operator (SD) and the reach (national). No personal LinkedIn — keeps focus on the company.

### GDK demo labeling
**Why:** The portfolio block was titled "Live Client Build" and the card badge read "Home Service Operator." Both implied GDK is a paying customer. It isn't — it's a live demo build engineered to show what a Velonyx system looks and feels like end-to-end. Misrepresenting it as a real client is a credibility risk if a prospect ever asks (and an integrity issue regardless). New labels: "Live Demo Build" / "See a Real Velonyx Build — Live Demo" / "Demo Build · Home Service" / "Explore the Live Demo →". Honest, still impressive, removes the awkward question.

### FAQ — revision policy specifics
**Why:** The previous answer was deliberately vague ("a capped set of revision rounds within a defined revision window") — that vagueness is what makes founders nervous about committing. Specific numbers + a clear after-window rate ($100/hour) shows we've thought about the edge case and aren't going to nickel-and-dime. "Most builds reach final approval within those 2 rounds" sets the expectation that 2 is genuinely enough, not a trick number designed to force overages.

### FAQ — new refund policy entry
**Why:** A $3,000 build with no visible refund policy is a conversion blocker on every cold prospect. Buyers Google "[vendor] refund policy" before buying anything in the $1k+ range. Burying it in Terms of Service only is hostile. Phase-tiered (Option E) is the most defensible model for custom-engineered work — full refund where no work is done, decreasing as the build progresses, zero after delivery. The FAQ entry summarizes; `/refund-policy.html` carries the full policy.

### JSON-LD address correction
**Why:** Accurate local SEO + accurate trust signal. Chula Vista 91914 is the real operating address (corrected from the placeholder "San Diego" used in earlier scaffolding per CLAUDE.md note). Adding `postalCode` to the JSON-LD strengthens local entity recognition. `sameAs` expansion to 3 URLs (Instagram + LinkedIn company + Google share link) gives search engines and AI crawlers more disambiguation surface area to confirm Velonyx is one cohesive entity. The Google share link in particular is what GBP exposes for entity confirmation — high-value for AI search citations.

### Refund Policy page
**Why:** Same root reason as the FAQ entry — answers the buyer's risk question in the buyer's own words, on a public page they can return to without re-asking. Phase-tiered structure matches how custom work actually behaves: hours spent can't be unspent. Mirroring the styling of `terms.html` keeps it visually anchored as a legal-tier document, not marketing copy. Chargeback paragraph + refund-request process protect both sides operationally.
