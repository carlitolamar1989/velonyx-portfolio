What "PII Compliant" Actually Means

PII = Personally Identifiable Information. Your customer's name, phone, email, address, payment info. The law has a lot to say about how you handle it. Here's the plain-English version of what we build into every site.

Why this isn't optional anymore

If you collect customer names, phone numbers, or emails on your website — you're a "data processor" under the law. California (where you're located), Virginia, Colorado, and most states have laws that say what you can and can't do with that info.

Real consequences for ignoring this:

  • Texting a customer without consent = up to $1,500 per text message (TCPA law)
  • Data breach with no notification = $7,500 per record in California (CCPA)
  • No privacy policy = $2,500 per violation per visitor (CCPA)
  • No "delete my data" button = same penalties + class action exposure

A single TCPA lawsuit over unauthorized texts can put a small business under. We build the safeguards in so you don't have to think about it.

The 4 things every compliant site needs

In plain English. No legalese.

1 · Information Travels Safely

When a customer fills out a form, the info doesn't get intercepted.

  • SSL / HTTPS encryption
    Like sending a sealed envelope instead of a postcard. The little padlock icon in the browser bar means this is on. We never ship a site without it.
  • Forms posted over secure connections only
    Customer's name and phone number can't be intercepted on public Wi-Fi at Starbucks.
  • Payment data never touches your server
    Stripe handles credit card data directly. You never see, store, or transmit a card number — which means PCI compliance is dramatically simpler.
  • No customer info in URLs or page source
    Some shoddy sites leak customer data in the web address itself. We don't.
2 · Customers Know What You're Collecting

Required by California, Virginia, and most other state laws.

  • Custom privacy policy on your site
    Plain-English explanation of what data you collect, why, who sees it, and how customers can opt out. Linked in the footer of every page.
  • Cookie consent banner
    The "Accept Cookies" pop-up you see on sites. Required because tracking pixels (like Google Analytics) ARE technically collecting data. We install it CCPA-friendly out of the box.
  • SMS opt-in checkbox before any text
    Federal law (TCPA) requires explicit consent BEFORE you text a customer. Penalty for skipping this is $500–$1,500 per unauthorized text. Our forms have the checkbox.
  • "Reply STOP to opt out" baked into every text
    Also required by TCPA. Customer can stop the texts any time. The system honors STOP automatically.
3 · Only the Right People See Customer Data

Available on Premium tier and up — not on every site by default.

  • Customer login (real authentication)
    Customer signs in with email + password. Without this, anyone with the portal URL could see anyone's info — which is why basic demo portals don't work for real production.
  • Role-based access
    Your office manager only sees what they need. Your tech only sees today's jobs. Your customer only sees their own account. Nobody sees more than they should.
  • Audit logs
    If a customer ever asks "who looked at my account?" you can answer with a date, a time, and a name. Required by HIPAA, recommended by every other framework.
  • Encrypted database (data at rest)
    Even if a hacker breaks into the server, the data they steal is gibberish without the encryption key. Like a locked safe inside a locked house.
4 · Customers Can Take Control of Their Data

California law (CCPA) requires this. Most states are following.

  • "Send me everything you have on me" button
    If a customer asks for a copy of all their data, the system can package it up and email them. Required under California's CCPA law.
  • "Delete me from your system" button
    Customer can request their data be deleted. Right-to-be-forgotten. The system honors it within 45 days as required.
  • "Don't sell my data" link in the footer
    California law specifically requires this link be visible on every page. We add it automatically.
  • Breach notification process
    If your data IS ever stolen, the law requires you to tell affected customers within 30–60 days depending on your state. We document the playbook so you know exactly what to do.

Which tier do you actually need?

Starter / Growth

If your customers fill out a form and you call them back — that's it. No portal. No customer login.

Examples: Solo barber. Single-tech HVAC. Personal trainer with Instagram-only marketing.

Premium

If customers log into a portal, see invoices, message you, view their service history.

Examples: Multi-truck plumbing or HVAC. Boutique gym with members. Photographer with client galleries. Trade business with admin dashboard.

Enterprise

If you handle anything regulated — health info (HIPAA), financial info (NYDFS), legal info (privilege).

Examples: Medspa, dentist, chiropractor, fertility clinic, attorney, financial advisor, multi-location franchise.
